Decoupling the network functions  MEC


The literature surrounding **Unmanned Traffic Management (UTM)** and the European **U-space** framework places a heavy focus on architectural performance—specifically balancing the capabilities of cloud computing against strict latency and data governance boundaries.

The following breakdown highlights how cloud architectures, communication protocols, and edge-computing structures perform within UTM systems based on recent literature.

### 1. Cloud Architecture Implementations in UTM

Cloud-based infrastructures are standard for managing wide-scale data processing in UTM. However, as systems transition from strategic planning (pre-flight) to real-time tactical deconfliction (in-flight), the "system of systems" approach introduces unique latency bottlenecks.

- **Centralized vs. Federated Solutions:** In European U-space design, architectures lean on a combination of a centralized **Common Information Service Provider (CISP)** acting as a single point of truth, and multiple localized **U-space Service Providers (USSPs)** managing operators. Industry software platforms, like DFS Deutsche Flugsicherung’s cloud-based UTM system _"ZEUS"_, utilize redundant, multi-stage private cloud environments to segregate development, testing, and live operational traffic data.
    
- **The "Ideal Pipe" Fallacy:** Centralized cloud setups often suffer from architectural limitations because simulations and models treat communication paths as an "ideal pipe." In high-density environments, structural latency constraints and network detachment risks pose critical safety vulnerabilities, as real-time tracking data can become choked at the cloud gateway.
    

### 2. Communication Protocol Benchmarks (Latency Results)

The choice of data-exchange protocols directly dictates whether a cloud-based architecture can meet stringent regulatory latency targets, such as the **10 ms** E2E delay required for tactical short-term conflict alerts (STCA).

Recent empirical evaluations comparing messaging frameworks within U-space drone detection pipelines show distinct latency profiles:

|**Protocol Paradigm**|**Target Frameworks**|**Latency Profile & Performance Insights**|
|---|---|---|
|**Push–Pull**|`REST-API`, `ZeroMQ`|**Lowest and most stable latency.** Ideal for direct U-space service links (e.g., flight plan confirmations and instant conformance tracking). Payload size increases have negligible impacts on transport speeds.|
|**Publish–Subscribe**|`MQTT`, `AMQP`|**Higher latency variance.** While useful for multi-agent synchronization and sensor streaming, payload intervals severely destabilize timing budgets under high-density message rates.|

### 3. Simulation & Stress-Testing Latency Limits

Because live stress-testing of dense urban airspace poses high safety risks, the literature relies heavily on microservice-based agent simulation platforms to measure system limitations.

- **Airspace Conformance & Risk Mitigation:** Microservice architectures split into two-level network models (simulating the physical mobile access layer separately from the core network aggregation backend) are used to stress-test high-density drone environments.
    
- **Strategic vs. Tactical Resolution:** Architectural simulation frameworks (such as those co-developed by Airbus and Eurocontrol) validate that standard cloud setups handling _volumetric intent_ (strategic deconfliction) can successfully eliminate up to **99.2% of mid-air collision risks** pre-flight. However, for real-time 4D trajectory updates and dynamic geofencing, cloud processing must rely heavily on **Mobile Edge Computing (MEC)** to bypass the propagation delays inherent to remote cloud databases.
