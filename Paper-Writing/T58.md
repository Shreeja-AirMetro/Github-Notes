<span style="color:rgb(0, 176, 80)">Advanced Air Mobility (AAM) - the idea of extending the cur- rent mobility landscape by transporting people and goods flex- ibly from A to B through the sky at low altitudes receives in- creasing interest in industry and academia. A core element are electrical-driven, highly automated aircrafts which are cap- able of vertical take-off and landing (commonly referred to as eVTOLs) (?). One key challenge for the safe operation of eVTOLs, generally small-scale UAVs, is the precise and reliable positioning and navigation. </span>

The navigation of AAM vehicles in VLL airspace demands high reliability as well, given their proximity to terrestrial infrastructure and the tighter separations between airspace users. However, satellite-based positioning in urban environments faces challenges such as radio interference, shadowing, and multi-path effects, necessitating the use of complementary technologies. Due to advancements in sensor technology, data processing, and sensor fusion, visual-inertial positioning can become a promising approach for ensuring the safe navigation of eVTOLs. Measurements from multiple visual sensors can be combined using Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) to augment onboard navigation capabilities. The traditional loop closure methods for drift correction can be adapted using geo-referenced landmarks and geodata-based multi-modal localization.

Navigation plays a vital role in transportation and mobility to reach the destination in a safe, efficient, and timely manner. Aircraft navigation aids in high-level tasks such as path and mission planning, while also addressing position-dependent factors like wind gusts, local airspace users, and fuel-efficient flight envelopes. Due to lower flight altitudes being limited to the vicinity of airports, additional positioning and landing infrastructure (like ILS?) has enabled higher resolution tracking for safe and reliable flights in the domain of civil manned aviation. On the other hand, the eVTOLs are proposed to fly at low altitudes predominantly, considering the European regulations for an unmanned aircraft to maintain an altitude within 120 m from the earth's surface. With the recent advances in deep learning, computer vision, and SLAM approaches, an onboard navigation approach can solve the ground infrastructure dependencies and also GNSS signal deterioration in urban canyons.

Radio-based navigation systems play a critical role in civil manned aviation, which have evolved from ground-based navigation (GBN) systems like VOR, DME, and DVORTAC to GNSS-based navigation systems. The former systems provide insufficient accuracy for low altitude flights, while the existing navigation systems based on GNSS suffer from inherent vulnerabilities like spoofing, jamming, and interference, especially low altitude flights are predisposed to shadowing in urban canyons. To enable safer flights, there is a need for a local and onboard navigation system.

<span style="color:rgb(0, 176, 80)">Advanced Air Mobility (AAM) represents a transformative shift in aviation, focusing on the integration of next-generation aircraft—such as remotely piloted, autonomous, and vertical take-off and landing (VTOL) vehicles—into the broader transportation ecosystem. These systems leverage cutting-edge technologies like electric and hybrid-electric propulsion, advanced air traffic management, and automation to enable new forms of passenger and cargo transport. AAM encompasses both urban air mobility (UAM), which addresses intra-city travel above congested roads, and regional air mobility (RAM), which connects suburbs, rural areas, and communities separated by challenging terrain or water[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_air_mobility)[2](https://accesspartnership.com/is-advanced-air-mobility-the-future-of-air-transportation/)[3](https://www.faa.gov/air_traffic/publications/atpubs/aim_html/chap11_section_6.html).</span>

<span style="color:rgb(0, 176, 80)">The vision for AAM is to create a safe, accessible, and sustainable air transportation network that can serve locations previously out of reach for traditional aircraft. This includes applications such as air taxis, drone-based cargo delivery, public services, and even private aerial vehicles. As the industry develops, it promises to alleviate ground traffic congestion, enhance connectivity, and reduce environmental impact through cleaner propulsion systems. While the sector is still in its early stages, significant investments from industry, government, and research organizations are accelerating its path toward commercialization and widespread adoption[2](https://accesspartnership.com/is-advanced-air-mobility-the-future-of-air-transportation/)[4](https://www.nasa.gov/mission/aam/)[5](https://www.michigan.gov/mdot/travel/mobility/aeronautics/advanced-air-mobility).

**Advanced Air Mobility (AAM): Main Challenges in Very Low Level (VLL) Airspace, High User Density, and Proximity to Ground Infrastructure**

**1. Traffic Management Complexity in VLL Airspace**

- **Variety of Users:** VLL airspace will host a mix of piloted, remotely piloted, fully autonomous AAM aircraft, as well as existing helicopters and drones. Ensuring safe coexistence and maximizing airspace performance requires new operational rules and coordination mechanisms[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    
- **On-Demand Operations:** The need to accommodate on-demand and highly dynamic flight operations increases the complexity of traffic management, especially as AAM scales up in urban environments[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    
- **Obstacle Density:** Proximity to buildings, power lines, and other urban infrastructure increases navigation complexity and collision risk[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    

**2. High Density of Users**

- **Air Traffic Congestion:** As AAM operations increase, so does the risk of congestion in both controlled and uncontrolled airspace, leading to greater demands on collision avoidance and communication systems[2](https://escholarship.org/content/qt0656t0dh/qt0656t0dh.pdf).
    
- **Conflict Management:** Managing arrival and departure times, slot allocation, and real-time deconfliction becomes more challenging as user density rises[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    
- **Integration with Existing Users:** Procedures must be established for safe interaction between AAM, commercial aircraft, and traditional drones, especially in transition zones between airspace classes[2](https://escholarship.org/content/qt0656t0dh/qt0656t0dh.pdf).
    

**3. Close Proximity to Ground Infrastructure**

- **Vertiport Availability:** The success of AAM depends on the availability and integration of launch and landing sites (vertiports) within existing urban infrastructure, requiring significant planning and investment[3](https://aviationweek.com/shownews/ebace/aam-bigger-just-aircraft-panelists-advise).
    
- **Ground Infrastructure Coordination:** Supplying reliable electricity for frequent eVTOL operations, integrating charging stations, and managing ground traffic around vertiports are significant logistical and technical challenges[3](https://aviationweek.com/shownews/ebace/aam-bigger-just-aircraft-panelists-advise).
    
- **Noise and Community Acceptance:** Operating close to residential and commercial areas raises concerns about noise emissions and public acceptance, necessitating alignment with community standards and expectations[4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0376042125000119)[3](https://aviationweek.com/shownews/ebace/aam-bigger-just-aircraft-panelists-advise).
    

**4. Technology and Regulatory Challenges**

- **Autonomy and Detect-and-Avoid:** The push for autonomous and BVLOS (Beyond Visual Line of Sight) operations means each vehicle may need advanced, costly detect-and-avoid systems, posing economic and technical burdens[5](https://www.autonomyglobal.co/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/aiaa-challenges-to-the-commercialization-of-AAM-DIGITAL-2-min.pdf).
    
- **Dynamic Airspace Management:** Traditional Visual Flight Rules (VFR) and current airspace management approaches will reach their limits as AAM scales, requiring dynamic, real-time airspace management and advanced UATM (Urban Air Traffic Management) services[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    
- **Flight Plan Authorization and Monitoring:** As operations scale, automated systems will be needed to review, authorize, and monitor flight plans, ensuring compliance and safety in real time[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    

**5. Integration with Air Traffic Management (ATM) and Unmanned Traffic Management (UTM)**

- **Coordination Across Systems:** Seamless integration and information sharing among ATM, UTM, and new UATM systems are critical to safely manage the increasing number and diversity of AAM operations in VLL airspace[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    
- **Scalability:** Existing airspace management systems may not scale to meet future commercial needs without significant redesign and new concepts of operation[5](https://www.autonomyglobal.co/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/aiaa-challenges-to-the-commercialization-of-AAM-DIGITAL-2-min.pdf)[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
    

These challenges highlight the need for a holistic approach to AAM deployment, involving not only aircraft certification and production but also robust ground infrastructure, advanced traffic management, regulatory adaptation, and community engagement[3](https://aviationweek.com/shownews/ebace/aam-bigger-just-aircraft-panelists-advise)[1](https://www.mlit.go.jp/koku/content/001739467.pdf).
