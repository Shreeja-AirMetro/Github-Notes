Academic Misconduct

Recommendation of the 185th plenum of the German Rectors' Conference from 6 July 1998  
(direct translation from German)

"Academic misconduct is said to occur when, in a scientifically important context, intentional or grossly negligent false information is given, the intellectual property of others is infringed upon or their research work is otherwise affected. The circumstances of the individual case play a decisive role.

  
In particular, the following can be considered examples of possible serious misconduct:  
  
a. false information [...]  
b. infringing on intellectual property [...]  
c. claiming the (co-)authorship of another without his or her consent [...]  
d. sabotaging research work (including damaging, destroying or manipulating experimental set-ups, devices, documents, hardware, software, chemicals or other things that another needs to carry out an experiment) [...]  
e. disposing of primary data [..], insofar as this violates legal provisions or discipline-related accepted principles of academic work."

C

Circulation Period

The time during which your doctoral work is available for the internal university community to evaluate after the submission of your dissertation is known as the circulation period.

  
"The right to inspection" (university-wide doctoral regulations § 9(5)) is specified in the faculty-specific doctoral regulations.

Cumulative Dissertation

Dissertations can consist of multiple previously published manuscripts as well as manuscripts that are soon to be in print. This type of publication is known as the cumulative or publication-based dissertation. Here it is important to clearly indicate the individual contribution of the doctoral candidate, for example, by providing information regarding the individual work contributed to each publication.

  
Previously published manuscripts are to be cited within the cumulative dissertation. The reverse is also true: unpublished manuscripts from the cumulative dissertation that are published at a later time must include a citation that refers to the dissertation.

D

Disputation

The disputation is the oral final examination of doctoral studies. The doctoral candidates present their doctoral projects and defend them in front of a expert audience. The strengths and also the weaknesses of the doctoral project are worked out and discussed.

  
See also "Rigorosum".

Doctoral Committee

according to § 2 of the GU university-wide doctoral regulations

  
Details about doctoral committees are specified in the doctoral regulations of the faculties. The faculties are responsible for the realization and implementation of the doctoral studies, in particular, for

  
a) accepting the doctoral student  
b) initiating the examination process  
c) appointing the referees  
d) forming the examination committee

  
The doctoral committee is composed of at least

  
a) three professors  
b) one research associate  
c) one student (at least in undergraduate, master's or doctoral studies)

  
The professors must make up the majority. Several faculties can together form a joint doctoral committee.

Doctoral Relationship

The doctoral relationship formally begins with the acceptance as a doctoral student in the faculty. From this point in time, the faculty-specific doctoral regulations apply.

However, during the admission procedure, there are already certain criteria that must be met, for example, providing proof of your completed university studies and specifying your supervisor.

E

Employment Relationship

The employment relationship is the contractually stipulated legal relationship between an employee and his or her employer.  
  
Doctoral candidates should clearly distinguish between the employment relationship and the research and supervisory relationship.

  
Among other things, the work contract regulates

  
● the job requirements (for example, the extent of the teaching obligations)  
● the length of employment  
● the number of working days, holidays and paid leave  
● the salary

Examination Committee

according to § 11 of the GU university-wide doctoral regulations

  
The examination committee is appointed by the doctoral committee and determines the candidate's performance during doctoral studies in a closed session.

  
The committee generally consists of the referees as well as an additional professor from the faculty of the doctoral candidate. The dissertation and the evaluations (and, if applicable, other documents) shall be made available to them.

  
In the case of cotutelle doctorates – binationally supervised doctoral studies at at least two universities – there are additional particularities. Further details can be found in the respective doctoral regulations for the faculty.

H

Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a scientific supposition. One can derive it inductively from acquiring knowledge through reading or observations or deduce it from laws or theories. Hypotheses are systematically and intersubjectively checked in a way that is comprehensible to others.

  
Depending on the particular scientific theoretical approach, at the end of the process, the hypothesis is rejected (falsifiability), verified (verifiability) or modified.

I

Inductive Approach

Academic knowledge is not only gained from checking expectations and hypotheses (deduction), but also from general conclusions drawn from observed phenomena and working through literature (induction). Some examples for inductively gained knowledge are the discovery of natural laws and the development of superordinate terms and theories.

P

Primary Literature

from Yale University Library Subject Guide ["Comparative Literature"](http://guides.library.yale.edu/content.php?pid=128822&sid=1187254) [accessed 10/12/2014]

"Primary sources are original materials on which other research is based. They are from the time period involved and have not been filtered through interpretation or evaluation. They are usually the first formal appearance of results in physical, print or electronic format. They present original thinking, report a discovery, or share new information."

"Secondary sources are less easily defined than primary sources. Generally, they are accounts written after the fact with the benefit of hindsight. They are interpretations and evaluations of primary sources. Secondary sources are not evidence, but rather commentary on and discussion of evidence. However, what some define as a secondary source, others define as a tertiary source. Context is everything."

R

Rigorosum

A rigorosum replaces the disputation as an oral final examination at some universities. Different than in a disputation, in a rigorosum, a critical stance not only adopted towards the dissertation topic, but other subjects are tested as well.

S

Search Alerts

Many online databases and search engines offer the possibility of receiving regular news about keywords (or a combination of keywords) by e-mail. These search alerts could be used, for example, to stay informed about the latest relevant research results. Some examples of this are PubMed Central and Google Alerts.

Supervisory Relationship

The supervisory relationship regulates the formal relationship between the doctoral candidates and their doctoral study supervisors. In general, both sides sign a joint supervisory agreement.

  
Among other things, the agreement covers

  
● times for regular meetings  
● length of doctoral studies  
● language of the dissertation  
● financial and professional support  
● latitude for qualification outside the subject area  
● talks about career perspectives

T

Touch-up Tools

Touch-up tools is the term used for the settings in graphics programmes that, as opposed to processing the overall image (for example, contrast or brightness), only make specific changes. In image editing within the framework of academic publications, such changes should be avoided or clearly specified.

  
Touch-up tools include, among others, cloning or healing tools. These programmes make it possible to replace one part of the image with another.
