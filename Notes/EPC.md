What’s particularly interesting from an IP perspective is that, while fundamental astrophysics itself is often not directly patentable, the tools, methods, and technologies that enable this research very often are. As you go through the course, you may find it useful to think about:  
Simulation methods and computational models — when might these become patentable versus remaining academic outputs?  
Instrumentation and data-processing techniques — many breakthroughs in astronomy are underpinned by innovations in sensors, imaging, and analysis  
Software and algorithms — where IP protection may involve a mix of patents, copyright, and sometimes trade secrets

Module 1 provides an **introduction to intellectual property rights (IPRs)**, why they exist and how they protect intangible assets.

IPR Types
Patent - grant 20 year - new and inventive 
2- utility model - shorter term - easier registration - conuntry based 
trade secret - not registered , not public, kept confidential (coke recipe)
trade mark - origin of goods , word, sound, logo, color - indefinite available
Design right - protect the outward appearance of the product - not tech function - new and diff - upto 25 years

Industrial property right 

Copyright is different - other of original founder sue / protect others from using (registered IPs) - available once any registration
